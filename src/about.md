# About the Project

## The Method

Our research uses **electron microscopy** to identify and characterise surface defects in engineering components. Electron microscopy offers resolution far beyond conventional optical microscopy, enabling us to visualise features at the micro- and nanometre scale.

The process involves preparing a sample from the engineering component and placing it in an electron microscope. A beam of electrons is directed at the surface, and the resulting interactions — such as secondary electron emission, backscattered electrons, or characteristic X-rays — are detected and translated into high-resolution images and compositional maps.

Two primary techniques are used:

- **Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)**: A focused electron beam scans across the surface, producing detailed topographic images. SEM is ideal for examining surface morphology, crack paths, corrosion pits, and wear patterns.
- **Transmission Electron Microscopy (TEM)**: Electrons pass through an ultra-thin sample, revealing internal structure at the atomic scale. TEM is used to study grain boundaries, dislocations, and nanoscale inclusions that may act as defect initiation sites.

In some cases, **Energy-Dispersive X-ray Spectroscopy (EDS)** is combined with SEM or TEM to determine the elemental composition of defects, helping to identify the origin of inclusions or the nature of corrosion products.

## What the Method Can Detect

The electron microscopy method is capable of identifying a wide range of surface and sub-surface defects, including:

- **Cracks**: Micro-cracks and fatigue cracks that may propagate under load.
- **Pitting**: Localised corrosion pits that can weaken the component surface.
- **Inclusions**: Non-metallic particles embedded in the material that can act as stress concentrators.
- **Scratches and gouges**: Mechanical damage from manufacturing or service wear.
- **Grain boundary anomalies**: Irregularities at the boundaries between crystalline grains that may affect material properties.
- **Surface roughness**: Deviations from intended surface finish specifications.

## Engineering Applications

The method has broad applicability across engineering sectors:

1. **Aerospace**: Inspecting turbine blades and critical airframe components for fatigue cracks and inclusions that could lead to in-service failure. The high resolution of electron microscopy allows detection of cracks at lengths well below the threshold for conventional non-destructive testing.

2. **Automotive**: Assessing engine components such as pistons, crankshafts, and gears for surface defects that could affect durability and performance. This supports both quality control during manufacturing and root-cause analysis after failure.

3. **Oil and Gas**: Examining pipeline steels and pressure vessel materials for stress corrosion cracking and hydrogen-induced cracking. Electron microscopy can reveal the microstructural features that make these materials susceptible to degradation in service environments.

4. **Power Generation**: Investigating steam turbine blades, boiler tubes, and nuclear reactor components for surface degradation mechanisms including erosion, corrosion, and thermal fatigue.

5. **Medical Devices**: Analysing the surface quality of surgical implants and instruments, where surface defects can affect biocompatibility and fatigue life.

6. **Additive Manufacturing**: Characterising the surface quality and microstructure of 3D-printed metal components, where defects such as lack-of-fusion and keyhole porosity can significantly affect mechanical properties.

## What the Method Can and Cannot Do

### Strengths

- **High resolution**: Features as small as a few nanometres can be resolved, far exceeding optical microscopy.
- **Detailed surface topography**: SEM produces three-dimensional-looking images that reveal surface morphology in detail.
- **Compositional analysis**: EDS provides elemental identification of defects and inclusions.
- **Permanent record**: Images and data are captured digitally for comparison over time.

### Limitations

- **Sample preparation**: Components must be sectioned and prepared, which is a destructive process — the method is not suitable for in-situ inspection of complete assemblies.
- **Small field of view**: The high magnification means only small areas can be examined at one time, making the method unsuitable for rapid screening of large surfaces.
- **Vacuum environment**: Electron microscopy requires a vacuum, so wet or volatile samples cannot be examined without specialised preparation.
- **Cost and time**: Electron microscopy is more expensive and time-consuming than conventional non-destructive testing methods such as ultrasonic or radiographic inspection.
- **Not a replacement for NDT**: The method complements, rather than replaces, conventional non-destructive testing. It is best used for detailed investigation of defects already identified by other methods, or for research and development purposes.

## Our Approach

At King's College London, our research group combines electron microscopy with advanced image analysis and machine learning techniques to automate the identification and classification of surface defects. This approach aims to reduce the time and expertise required for defect analysis, making the method more accessible to industrial partners.

We work with a range of engineering materials, including steels, titanium alloys, nickel-based superalloys, and aluminium alloys. Our facilities include field-emission SEM and TEM instruments equipped with EDS and electron backscatter diffraction (EBSD) for comprehensive microstructural characterisation.
